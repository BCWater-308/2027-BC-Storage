Where the Losses Happen — A Drought-Conditioned Look at the 2027 BC RMS Network

May 2026 · Prepared by AGUBC · 28 Voronoi polygons (13 North · 2 Chico · 13 South) · 28 2027 GWL RMS wells · polygon-by-polygon Sy from DWR SVSim Texture Data · WY 1999–2025 · ΔGWE × Syp × Areap, sliced by hydrologic condition and read against a project-portfolio sustainability target.

Two questions: when and where is the basin losing water, and what would it take to be sustainable by 2042? Across WY 1999–2025, loss isn't a steady year-over-year decline — it's sharply concentrated in Critical and Dry water-year types, with Wet and Above-Normal years doing the recovery work. The basin's net deficit is 146k AF — about 0.91% of the 16+ MAF in basin storage. Assuming the next 17 years see a roughly similar mix of year types as WY 1999–2025, holding current conditions through 2042 takes a basin-wide 6,322 AF/yr of new recharge or demand reduction; a project portfolio totaling 14,500 AF/yr (online by ~2032) would cover that with room to spare.

The picture in one sentence. Across 1999–2025, Critical and Dry years removed about 441,968 AF, Below-Normal years moved storage by -37,848 AF, and Wet + Above-Normal years recovered +334,085 AF. Net basin deficit through 2025: -145,732 AF, summed across all 28 polygons.

Method, in brief

Per polygon: ΔStoragep,y = (GWEp,y − GWEp,baseline) × Syp × Areap. GWEp,y is the polygon's 2027 GWL RMS well's spring composite (March mean for SWN-named wells; Feb–Apr mean for CWSCH wells), Good-quality DWR records only. In the 28-polygon network there is exactly one RMS well per polygon — the well that seeded the polygon's Voronoi cell. Each polygon is anchored to WY 1999 if it has a Good spring composite that year; otherwise to the polygon's first observation after 1999. We then take the per-polygon cumulative storage time series, compute year-over-year deltas (distributing multi-year DWR gaps evenly), and bucket each year by its official Sacramento Valley Index water-year type.

Specific yield is polygon-by-polygon, derived from DWR's SVSim Texture Data (Sacramento Valley Simulation Model v1.0, CKAN resource 544623e2-0cd5-4c5b-827f-affa4abf4e16): coarse-grained sediments → Sy = 0.15, fine-grained → Sy = 0.05, Syp = (% coarse × 0.15) + (% fine × 0.05) over the 0–500 ft below ground surface analysis window across the polygon's boreholes (≥200 ft of valid lithology required per borehole). Polygon Sy values for the 28-polygon network range 0.0594 to 0.1172; basin area-weighted mean is roughly 0.087.

Three polygons (23N02W25C001M, 23N01E29P002M, 21N01E25K001M) have insufficient SVSim borehole coverage (≤2 boreholes, none meeting the 200-ft valid-thickness threshold) and use the basin area-weighted mean as a Sy fallback. These are flagged in the table below with "(mean)". This is mostly a function of the new polygons being smaller and more numerous than the 17-polygon set, leaving some cells with thin SVSim coverage.

Year-type classification uses DWR's Sacramento Valley Index (Northern Sierra 8-Station Index) — the same five-tier system DWR publishes annually:

  • Wet: 1999, 2006, 2011, 2017, 2019, 2023, 2025 (7 years)
  • Above Normal: 2000, 2003, 2005, 2024 (4 years)
  • Below Normal: 2004, 2010, 2012, 2016, 2018 (5 years)
  • Dry: 2001, 2002, 2007, 2009, 2013, 2020 (6 years)
  • Critical: 2008, 2014, 2015, 2021, 2022 (5 years)

WY 2025 is preliminary (DWR upgraded to Wet in April 2025; final May-1 designation to verify). The 1991–2020 long-term 8SI average is 53.2 inches; SVI thresholds: Wet ≥ 9.2 MAF; Above Normal > 7.8 ≤ 9.2; Below Normal > 6.5 ≤ 7.8; Dry > 5.4 ≤ 6.5; Critical ≤ 5.4.

Putting the deficit in proportion

Why this matters for project design. Losses are concentrated in Critical and Dry years; recovery happens in Wet and Above-Normal years. But the project portfolio doesn't have to follow that pattern year by year — it's permanent infrastructure (recharge facilities, surface-water deliveries) that produces yield year after year. Annual yields will vary with hydrology, but the long-run average matters more than year-by-year matching: with portfolio yield at ≈14,500 AF/yr against a basin loss rate of ≈6,322 AF/yr, surplus from Wet and Above-Normal years carries through Critical ones. The goal isn't to offset every drought-year loss in the year it happens; it's to install enough permanent supply that the multi-year trend bends back toward equilibrium.

The 2042 sustainability target — hold current conditions, with the project portfolio

The Vina Subbasin has lost storage across the WY 1999–2025 record through 5 Critical years, 6 Dry years, 5 Below-Normal years, and a long-term decline rate of about 0.07%/yr. The basin's historic low was the WY 2022 trough at roughly 285,567 AF below baseline — and even at that low, no SGMA sustainability indicator registered an undesirable result.

The framing here is therefore not "recover to the WY 1999 baseline." The operational target is: GWE at each Groundwater Level RMS well stays at or above its WY 2022 level going forward. Minimum thresholds (MTs) for the GSP would be set lower than WY 2022, creating operational range and margin for an unforeseen 3–5 year drought. The portfolio's job is to ensure the next major drought doesn't push observed GWE below WY 2022, with projects coming online in roughly 3–5 years (by ~2032 at the latest).

The sizing test below uses the basin's average annual loss rate as the volumetric need — a stronger target than "stay above WY 2022" alone, because matching average loss keeps the basin near today's WY 2025 level (which sits ≈139,835 AF above the WY 2022 trough) and provides built-in cushion against drought. Per polygon, the volume needed to hold steady is just the polygon's average annual loss rate. Surplus polygons need zero. Sum across the 28 polygons:

Caveats

  • Specific yield is polygon-by-polygon from SVSim Texture Data (range 0.0594–0.1172); basin area-weighted mean is ≈0.087. The textural classifier (coarse vs. fine) is documented in scripts/build_sy_svsim.py and is reproducible from the DWR CKAN dataset. Three polygons (23N02W25C001M, 23N01E29P002M, 21N01E25K001M) lack sufficient SVSim borehole density and use the basin area-weighted mean as a fallback — a known limitation when fragmenting the basin into 28 smaller cells.
  • Baseline asymmetry. Late-baseline polygons — 23N01E07H001M (2019); 23N01E33A001M (2003); 23N01W03H004M (2012); 23N01W10M001M (2002); 23N01W28M004M (2009); 23N01W31M004M (2009); 22N01E20K001M (2000); 22N01E09B001M (2002); 20N02E24C001M (2000); 21N02E18C003M (2011); 20N01E02H003M (2002); 20N02E08H003M (2008); 20N02E09G001M (2002); 20N03E33L001M (2000); 21N01E13L004M (2012); 21N02E26E006M (2008); 21N02E32E001M (2009); 21N03E32B001M (2000) — have shorter records. 21N02E18C003M's large apparent surplus is partly an artifact of starting near the bottom of the 2011 drought; it cannot register pre-2011 drawdown.
  • Sustainable yield = 233,500 AF/yr per the 2022 GSP (Dec 15, 2021), p. ES-5: 243,500 AFY historical pumping minus 10,000 AFY decrease in storage. Used here as a denominator only; volumetric needs (AF/yr) do not depend on it.
  • Total storage = 16+ MAF per the GSP, BBGM-2020 estimate. Used in the proportion visual; not a calibration input.
  • Sustainability target. "Sustainability" here means GWE at each Groundwater Level RMS well stays at or above its WY 2022 level going forward — the basin's historic low, at which no sustainability indicator registered an undesirable result. Minimum thresholds (MTs) for the GSP would be set lower than WY 2022 to create operational range and margin in the event of a 3–5 year drought. The volumetric sizing in this dashboard uses the basin's average annual loss rate as the need, which is a stronger target than "stay above WY 2022" alone — matching average loss keeps the basin near WY 2025 conditions, providing built-in drought cushion.
  • Project portfolio is at-yield, not at-design. Conjunctive-use figures assume successful surface-water substitution. Recharge figures assume 2–3 storm events per year where excess flow can be diverted into recharge basins; drought-year recharge rates will be lower, wet-year rates higher.
  • Per-polygon vs. basin-wide. The portfolio covers basin loss with margin, but 14 polygons remain locally short. The two Chico cells (22N01E09B001M and 22N01E20K001M) together are the largest residual shortfall — Chico may be able to add recharge capacity by directing more flow into Lindo Channel, but evaluating that potential is outside this scope. Whether one polygon's neighbors' surplus reaches its RMS well depends on lateral hydraulic conductivity that this dashboard does not model.
  • Hold-current target assumes a representative year-type mix going forward. The 6,322 AF/yr basin avg loss rate is the average over WY 1999–2025, which had 6 Wet, 4 Above Normal, 5 Below Normal, 6 Dry, and 5 Critical years. If WY 2026–2042 sees materially more Critical years, the realized loss rate will be higher; if more Wet years, lower. The portfolio's recovery margin (≈8,178 AF/yr) is the buffer for that uncertainty.
  • Year-type definitions are the official Sacramento Valley Index types, published annually by DWR from the Northern Sierra 8-Station Index.
  • Comparison to the 17-polygon version. The basin deficit through WY 2025 is smaller here (-145,732 AF vs. −193,010 AF in the 17-polygon build), and the avg loss rate is smaller too (6,322 AF/yr vs. 8,558). The difference traces to (a) the new 21N02E18C003M polygon being larger and posting a recovering surplus, (b) the new Chico cells being smaller than the old dissolved Chico polygon, (c) the addition of late-baseline polygons that contribute less to early-record buckets, and (d) different per-polygon Sy values from the SVSim recompute against the new geometry.
